INTERVIEW ETIQUETTE

4.1 Objectives

1. To introduce the process of interview.

2. To provide the tips for success in various types of interviews.

3. To introduce various questions commonly asked in interviews with necessary clues.

4. To provide guidelines related to pre interview, during interview and after interview Preparations

4.2 Interview Etiquette

While attending for an interview, we have to bear in mind some important points to come out in flying colors. The success lies greatly upon the planning, preparation and delivery with good presence of mind. Interview etiquette and good manners are essential. Etiquette is the code for polite behavior in a society. It is a code the generally interviewer expects .The etiquette of interviews has many elements but they may have inexpressive of what is done in polite society and not relevant to everyday life. However it is very important to follow the right etiquette in a job interview to impress the interviewers.

4.2.1 Interview dress

The way of presentation for an interview is one of the major elements in creating impression. Often it is wise to dress decently and conservatively. Before the interview, try to assess the place and the people and gain a good idea of what is best. In particular it is wise not to dress provocatively. It has been shown that people who dress in a conservative suit are generally more successful for professional jobs.

4.2.2 Be punctual

It is good to plan arrive close to the location of the prospective employer well in time. Even try to find the route beforehand. It is possible to reach the reception point a few minutes early and be in time for the interview. Any last minute rush will make you agitated and stressed and you may not be able to present well in the interview.

4.2.3 Good handshake

It is customary to shake hands, when we meet people. A handshake says much about the person. A limp handshake by a clammy hand will not impress. A firm handshake gives a better impression. It is normal to be nervous before the interview, and this may result in a sweaty hand.

4.2.4 Non-verbal communications:

Often we think of verbal communication, convey a lot of information. Non-verbal account creates good impression with anyone at the interview. Eye contact shows that you are interested in what the other person is saying and doing and that you have confidence. Posture is also another indicator. Comfortable Sitting in a chair gives an impression of a casual attitude and it gives a "don't care" impression .It is important to make proper eye contact with both or all interviewers, and to sit straight in a chair. It is not always possible to "fake" the body language, so feel yourself positive and attentive before going in for the interview. Proper body language indicates the right attitude of the candidate.

4.2.5 Sell yourself;

We are sending a resume furnishing with all details means; we are ready to sell our skills in the job market. The hiring manager assess our skills based on the details mentioned in the resume and the answers for the questions during interview and other criteria set by the board members .

The interviewer expects that you are selling your skills. It is effectively part of the job interview etiquette to be able to sell yourself in a professional and sensible manner. Use examples and specifics to be truthful about your accomplishments and achievements. Detail the reasoning for decisions you made and the ideas that help you to be successful in a particular area.

4.2.6 Don't dominate the conversation:

It is necessary to talk in a positive way about yourself. You have to allow the conversation be in two-way. The interviewer asks questions, so be aware of this so that they can get into the conversation. It is also necessary not to be seen as boastful.

4.2.7 Be truthful:

To be truthful is one of the interview etiquette, if an interviewer identifies that you are lying, you will not be given job. Honesty impacts how much you can claim you have done yourself.

4.2.8 Thank you

To end on the right note at the end of the interview is a short thank you, will help the interview. It is also good writing a short note after the interview - possibly that evening - to thank the interviewers.

4.3 Definition

Interview is a two party affair and the interaction between them is a process. It is conducted to have adequate information from the candidate. Apt information is not obtained, if interview is not handled carefully. There are different definitions for the word interview. It is an exchange of ideas, the answering of questions and communication between two parties. The interview provides details of education, training, previous experience, conversational ability, family background, inward and outward strength.

4.4 Objectives of Conducting Interview:

1. The available information in the application form is thoroughly checked.

2. To obtain additional information from the applicant’s interview is conducted.

3. To provide interviewee with necessary informations about the job.

4. To establish understanding between the organization and the candidate interview is a stage.

4.5 Do's and Don'ts in interviews:

1. Look decently.

2. Dress neatly and appear formally.

3. Be punctual and present before the specified time.

4. Express your views clearly relevant to the point.

5. Attend with all required documents, if necessary you can refer them.

6. Listen to the questions attentively and answer clearly and thoughtfully.

7. Make eye-contact as it speaks a lot.

8. Maintain apt body language as it indicates inward appearance.

Don’ts

1. Speaking lies. It says immediately irregularity in work and behavior.

2. Using cell phone during the interview which says inattentiveness and recklessness.

3. Appearing arrogant in voice and appearance.

4. Dressing inappropriately.

5. Not making eye contact.

4.3. Tips to improve Interview Etiquette;

1. Very first greet your interviewers in the board or committee.

2. Next look people in the eyes of the interview members and give a smile.

3. Give firm handshake to have acquaintance and avoid fear.

4. Listen to the conversation completely and let them speak sufficiently.

Some model interview question and clues:

1. Say something about yourself?

Give necessary information concisely and that shows exactly how you’re fit for the job. Don’t give your complete history either related to personal or employment. Start with the 2-3 specific experiences.

2. How did you come to know about the position?

If you got information about through a reliable source say it then share why you were so excited about it. If you discovered the company through an event or article, share it with them. Show your passion for and connection to the company.

3. Do you know anything about the company?

Know the company's goals, using a couple of key words and phrases from the website, but then go on to make it personal. Say, “I really believe in this approach because…” and give a personal example or two relevant to the context.

4. Why are you desirous for this job?

You should have a good answer for this question because companies want to take people who are passionate about the job. First, identify a couple of valid points that make the role a fit for you, and then share why you love the company.

5. What is the use in hiring you?

There's no better option for you to sell your skills to the hiring manager. Your job here is to craft an answer that covers three things that you can do the work, you can deliver great results and that you'll really fit in with the team and culture; and that you'd be a better hire than any of the other candidate.

6. What are the greatest professional strengths do you have?

Choose your strengths that are quite suitable to this particular position. Be accurate and relevant.

7. Explain about a challenge or conflict you've faced at work and how did you deal with it.

Every interviewee seem nice and pleasant in a job interview but what will happen if you’re hired and Gladys in compliance starts getting in your face. Again, use the good method being sure to focus on how you handled the situation professionally and productively and ideally close with a happy ending like how you came to a resolution or compromise.

8. In coming five years where do you see yourself?

Be clear about your future goals, but consider this: A hiring manager wants to know a) if you've set realistic expectations for your career, b) if you have ambition and c) if the position aligns with your goals and growth and if the position isn’t necessarily a one-way ticket to your aspirations? It’s good to say role in helping you make that decision.

9. Why are you going to leave your present job?

Often this question is asked. Keep things positive—Instead, frame things in a way that shows that you're eager to take on new opportunities and that the role you’re interviewing for is a better than your present position.

10. What is your expectation in a new position?

I satisfy with what this position has to offer is the good answered. Be clear in it.

11. What type of work environment do you prefer?

Suggestive answer is one that’s similar to the environment of the company you're applying to.

12. When did you disagree with a decision that was made at work?

Everyone disagrees with the boss occasionally, but in asking this interview question, hiring managers want to know that you can do so in a productive, professional way. “You don’t want to tell the story about the time when you disagreed but your boss was being a jerk and you just gave in to keep the peace. Give the answer where your actions made a positive difference on the outcome of the situation, whether it was a work-related outcome or a productive working relationship and a more effective.

13. What is the reason for a gap in your employment?

If you were unemployed for a period of time, be direct and to the point about what you’ve been up to. Then, start the conversation toward how you are able to do the job satisfactorily with the entire organization and contribute to the growth of organization.

14. What are the reasons to change your career paths?

Why you've made the career decisions describe briefly to the hiring manager with a few examples of how your past experience is transferrable to the new role. This doesn't have to be a direct connection; in fact, it's often more impressive when a candidate can make seemingly irrelevant experience seem very relevant to the role. It also indicates that the candidates interest in different sections in the same domain.

15. When you come across pressure or stressful situations how do you manage or deal with?

Face a stressful situation head-on in a productive, positive manner and let nothing stop you from reaching your goals.

16. How much salary you are expecting?

You come up with some expectation and we recommend stating the highest number in that range that applies, based on your experience, education and skills. Then, make sure the hiring manager knows that you're flexible. You know your skills are valuable but you want the job and are willing to negotiation in that way you should convey your idea.

4.6 Job interview content:

Interview questions are set to know the applicants attributes that are related to the job applied. It is divided into three types, general traits, experiential factors, and core job elements. The first type is relatively stable applicant traits such as mental ability, personality, interests, goals and values. The second type is job knowledge and the third type refers to the knowledge, skills, and abilities connected to the job. Unstructured and structured interviews:

These types are conducted by some organizations depend up on the company norms and system.

4.6.1 Unstructured

Unstructured interviews are as the name indicates they are not structured. The interviewer can change questions as he feels is best and different interviewer’s rate applicant responses in different ways. There are also no directions put in place regarding how the interviewer and the interviewee should interact before, during or after the interview. When two interviewers conduct an interview with the same person may not agree and see the candidate the same way even in the same interview with that applicant. Unstructured interviews may fail to identify the high-quality candidates for the job.

4.6.2 Structured

Interview structure is the degree to which interviews are similar and conducted the same across applicants. They are also called guided, systematic, or patterned interviews. Structured interviews aim to make both the content and the assessment the same no matter what applicant is being interviewed. Specifically, researchers commonly address 15 elements that can be used to make the interview's content and evaluation process similar Content structure:

4.7 Types of questions

Interviewers ask applicants types of questions. Two major types are used situational questions and behavioral questions. The requirement for the job are critical incidents but more or less relevant tasks and can be collected through interviews or surveys with current employees, managers, or subject matter experts. Combining both situational and behavioral questions into the interview to get the best of both question type.

4.7.1 Situational interview questions:

In a situational interview questions are given the candidate a set of circumstances and then indicate how they would respond in that situation, the questions are future oriented.

4.7.2 Behavioral interview questions:

1. How you can successfully convince someone to bring to your view, describe a situation.

2. When did you set a goal and achieve it? Explain with an example?

3. Share your experience when you had to use your presentation skills to influence someone's opinion.

4. Share your experience when you had to conform to a policy with which you did not agree.

4.7.3 Stress interview questions:

1. As part of the selection process, Candidates are asked to make a presentation. One stress question is that the applicant is given 20 minutes to prepare a presentation and then come back to room five minutes later and demand that the presentation is given immediately. This is stressful and is useful as a predictor of how the candidate will perform the job in such conditions.

2. If your colleague is caught by you cheating on the company purchase, what is your response?

3. What is your opinion about this going on interview?

4. Well, if that's the best answer you can say ‘yes’.

4.7.4 Technical interview question:

Technical interviews focus on creativity and problem solving skills. The questions enable interviewee's problem-solving skills and show their ability in solving the challenges faced in the job through creativity.

4.8 Interview Strategies:

There are two main categories in nonverbal behaviors. The first one is vocal cues such as articulation, pitch, fluency, frequency of pauses, speed, etc. and visual cues that are smiling, eye contact, body orientation and lean, hand movement, posture, etc. Applicants who engage in positive nonverbal behaviors such as smiling and leaning forward are perceived as more likable, trustworthy, credible, warmer, successful, qualified, motivated, competent and socially skilled. It is careful, communicating through the nonverbal behaviors. People who are physically attractive tend to have positive initial impressions. Perceive the person to be smart, socially competent, and have good social skills and general mental health.

General questions are viewed more positively than situational or behavioral questions and 'puzzle' interview questions are negative being perceived unrelated to the job, unfair or vague how to answer.

2. Does this person demonstrate honesty, integrity, and a good work ethic?

3. What motivates this person?

5. Will they focus on tasks and stick to the job until it is done?

4.9 Preparation before, during and after interview:

4.9.1 Before an interview:

1. Get a good sleep the night before. Look better and feel better to make the first impression.

2. Know the company details to answer the questions in the right context. .

3. Eat a good breakfast to look more alert and focused.

4. Prepare questions and know the answers to basic questions that they will most likely ask you.

5. Know your strengths and prepare a list of them.

6. Prepare a solid list of references to give your interviewer.

7. Turn off your cell phone.

4.9.2 During an interview:

1. Answer the questions precisely and clearly.

2. Shake the interviewer’s hand freely.

3. Let the interviewer show your chair. It is the time to be submissive.

4. Take a second before answering a question.

5. Take down the valid points.

6. Let your achievements speak for you.

7. Ask full description of the type of work that you will be doing.

8. Keep eye contact.

9. Start conversation topics with which you are comfortable.

10. Don’t talk too quickly.

11. Answer questions directly.

12. Don’t use slang.

13. Don’t be arrogant.

14. Be not nervous.

4.9.3 After interview:

1. Drop off your thank you note .Email them after you drop off your note.

2. Create a list of items that you did well and that you would like to improve on.

3. Follow up appropriately.

4. If you were told there was a specific date to expect a response, wait until that date.
