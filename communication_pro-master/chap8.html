Tips for Effective PowerPoint Presentations:

To create a consistent and simple design template the slide master feature should be used.

The change of content presentation is fine. Maintain other elements such as font, colors, and background. Simplify the language and limit the number of words on each screen.

Use important phrases and include only essential information. For text and background use contrast colours. On a light background, dark text looks good.

These features may seem impressive at first, but are distracting and get old quickly. Good quality images should be used that reinforce and complement your message. Only "build" screen makes your point, because they can slow your presentation.

Limit the number of slides. An important rule is that one slide per minute. Learn to direct your presentation in a non-linear fashion. Know how to and practice moving forward and backward within your presentation.

If possible, view your slides on the screen you are using for your presentation, when audiences ask to see the previous screen again. Make sure they are readable from the back row seats. Text and graphics should be in a considerable size to read, but not so large as to appear "loud." In the event of technical difficulties have a plan?

Remember that transparencies and handouts will not show animation or other special effects. Practice with someone who did not see your presentation. Ask them for fair feedback about colors, content and graphics you‘ve included.     Introduction, body and conclusion:-

  • Presentations should be direct and logical. It is important that you avoid complex structures and explain and discuss your work clearly for which much attention need to be paid. An ideal structure for a presentation includes:
  • The introduction part should be a welcoming and informative.
  • The body which consists of main points should be presented in a coherent series and in a logical sequence;
  • The conclusion should be purposeful and clear.
  • These elements are discussed below.

    • The introduction

    The introduction is the first part in which the presenter states the purpose and gives the presentation details. This is a very important part of your talk as it gains the audience‘s interest and confidence. The Key elements of an effective introduction are

    A positive start: ―Good morning , my name is Rahul and …‖;

    A statement of what is to be discussed: ―My topic for today‘s presentation is…‖

    A statement of the treatment to be applied to the topic (e.g. to compare, contrast, evaluate, describe): ―I am going to compare the three main principles of …‖

    A statement of the outcomes of the presentation: ―I hope this will provide us with …‖

    A statement of what the audience should do (e.g. when they can ask questions or whether they will need to take notes or not): ―I will distribute a handout that summarizes my presentation before taking questions at the end.‖

    Aim to speak your beginning confidently (wait until the audience is quiet before you start speaking) and communicate your topic with energy and enthusiasm.

    Main points

    The main points are the key points of your talk. They play a vital role to prioritize, focus and sequence your information. When planning your presentation, produce a summary of the main points that you would like to make, use a few words and short sentences to express each point. Ask yourself: ―what am I really telling them? What should they be learning here?‖ Your answers to these questions will help youcommunicate clear and effective messages to your audience.

    After identifying your main points, you should beautify them with supporting information. For example, add clarity to your argument through the use of diagrams, illustrate a link between theory and practice, or confirm your claims with appropriate data. Use the supporting information to add color and interest to your talk, but avoid detracting from the clarity of your main points by overburdening them with too much detail.

    Transitions:

    Transitions help the audience to guide their way through your presentation. They divide into sub- sections, link different aspects of your talk and show progression through your topic. Importantly, transitions draw the audience‘s attention to the process of the presentation as well as its content. Examples include:

    ―I am to begin by discussing …‖

    ―Now that we have explored the ... I would like to move on to …‖

    ―In contrast to my earlier statements related …‖

    ―Moving away from a focus on.…‖

    Without speaking Transitions can be made. Non-verbal transitions include pausing, changing a slide or other visual aid, moving to a different area of the room before resuming speaking, or making eye contact with a different group in the audience.

    The Conclusion:

    The conclusion is an essential t underdeveloped section of a presentation. This is the stage at which you can summarize the content and purpose of your talk, Give an overview of what has been achieved and make a lasting impression. Important elements of a conclusion are:

    A review of the topic and purpose of your presentation: ―In this presentation I wanted to explore

    A statement of the conclusion to be drawn from your work: ―I hope to have been able to show that the effect of ‖

    An indication of the next stages: ―This does of course highlight the need for further research in the area of …‖

    An instruction as to what happens next (questions, discussion or group work?): ―I would now like to give you the opportunity to ask questions …‖

    a thank you to the audience for their attention and participation: ―That‘s all I have time for. Thank you very much for listening.‖

    As with your introduction, you should try to speak to the listeners directly during your conclusion, consolidating the impression of a confident and useful presentation.

    A presentation needs a well defined structure to make the most impressive.. This should centre on a series of identifiable main points that are supported by appropriate detail. Use transitions to link and move between points, helping your listeners to understand the development or your argument. An introduction and conclusion are important parts of your presentation. They help you to establish a clear purpose for your talk at the start and summarize your main points before you finish speaking.

    How to Rehearse Your Speech

    ―Rehearsing even one time will improve your confidence in your material.‖

    You might practice for 60 hours. You might practice for 60 minutes. Either way, here are a few tips that will help to have maximum benefit from rehearsing that means it is not time consuming process.

  • Re-create the speech setting unless you are preparing for a webcast. Mere reading your speech before podium is not ideal. Try to duplicate the speech setting as much as you can.
  • Practice in the room or in a corner or where you feel comfort to practice.
  • Stand up. To project realistic voice stand up and speak.
  • Practice with props and visual aids.
  • Arrange an audience. Practicing with an audience is better than practicing without one… even they are not your target audience.
  • Think of what you are going to wear, when you are going to deliver your speech. is it going to add complications? Inhibit gestures or movement in any way?

    • Take notes

    Don‘t hesitate to stop in the middle of your rehearsal to write down the ideas as they come to you. Understand the internal feelings immediately.

    Experiment:

    Try out different voices, gestures, or staging. This is especially important for your opening, conclusion and any other key points. Give yourself confidence knowing that these lines will be delivered exactly as you intended.

    Adjust Time yourself

    You can easily do this, but it helps if someone else can time you. Insert plan where to insert pauses, and insert delays when you expect laughter or audience response. This may feel funny, but accurate time estimation will give you idea if more editing is needed.

    Use, all that you learn to edit your speech and make it better. Taking Feedback:

    ―After the rehearsal, take feedback. Make it clear that you want honest opinions about what is to be improved to make a good presentation.

    o It is the best practicing your speech with someone who will give you honest feedback.

    o Valuable feedback is given when practicing with an audience.

    o Your humour is drawing smiles and laughs or it is missing completely?

    o Are you keeping the audience‘s attention completely?

    o There are different ways of giving feedback.

    o Are you receiving positive feedback in the form of nodding heads and smiles, or is it a blank stare the most common expression?

    o After the rehearsal, actively collect feedback. Make it clear that you want fair opinions about improvements. A dozen ―Good speech!‖ comments may encourage you, but it won‘t improve the quality of your speech. To reap feedback that will improve your speech, ask open-ended questions like these:

    o What was your preferred element in the speech? Why?

    o What would you like to see improved?

    o How can I improve my speech for next time?

    o This is far better than asking yes/no questions such as ―Did you like it?‖

    If the presentation is important to you, and you don‘t have a test audience that provides you with valuable feedback, hire a coach! Hire me or one of thousands of speech coaches.

    The Do's and Don'ts of Good Presentations:

  • Do plan your presentation before you start in PowerPoint - outline your story and the key slides, their objectives and key messages you want to cover.
  • Do use work that other person have already completed, but be cautious of using other people‘s slides directly without correcting them, are it up to date and is the slide still effective. Make sure that when you use other people‘s slide/info that you adjust them to the look & feel and tone of voice of your presentation.
  • Do send your presentation to interested parties for feedback where appropriate before you present. They may be able to pick up on errors/missing info or add additional info/insight to your presentation. The best presentations are rarely created by one individual. Remember what you are communicating.
  • Do use a consistent look and feel throughout the presentation. When working on corporate presentations ensure that you are following the presentation and specified guidelines.
    • Is your presentation using the corporate stationery/template, and using the correct tone of voice? Large corporations often have Tone of Voice Guidelines, which are either part of the Brand/Presentation Guidelines, or part of a guideline suite.
  • Do with relevant graphics and images break up in presentation will be apt and attractive. Everybody is not interested, but many people are more visually stimulated and therefore need to show images or graphical representations to understand as well as just the words on the screen.
  • Do use font size of 14 pt or larger – smaller fonts may mean that people will not be able to read it. If it can‘t be read, it shouldn't be on your slide.
  • Do use bullets. Do not use paragraphs. People find it very hard to read off of the screen. Paragraphs make people instinctively switch off and disengage with a presentation as they difficult to read.
  • Do maintain gap between lines. This helps to make them easier to read and makes them look neater on the screen. ―Presentation‖ look is as important as the content when communicating.
  • Do use maximum 15 slides for a 30 min presentation.
  • Do, the best presentations are often the ones where you have a bit of fun and engage with your audience.
  • Do use handouts – especially for detailed information and complicated diagrams. Depending upon the style of your presentation you can choose to give them either before or during or after your presentation.
  • You should indicate in the speaker notes that there are handouts if it is a cascade presentation and these should be delivered to the audiences.
  • Do create detailed speaker notes using bullet format rather than paragraph to make them easier.
  • You should provide the following on each of your slide.
  • Don‘t assume that the level of knowledge of your audience is same around the details as you do. This is very important when creating cascade presentations and it is important that you provide sufficient detail in speaker notes for the lowest level of knowledge.

    • Don‘t over script your presentation

    Keep the presentation simple, use bullets in your speaker notes and break it down using the framework above, when it is likely to be used by others

    By using bullets you allow a presenter the opportunity to pick out key information easier and therefore present it more naturally

  • Don‘t create a presentation which doesn‘t allow time for questions or queries, remember communication is two way. if no one chipped in nothing at all, It is not a good presentation.
  • Don‘t dump all your ideas onto PowerPoint as a stream of consciousness. Don‘t use more than 12 words per bullet point on your slides and where possible avoid this on your speaker notes – keep it clean and simple.
  • On speaker notes when you have more than one sentence or message per bullet break it up with clear line breaks.
  • Don‘t overuse sound and animation – animation that is used unnecessary is irritating to the audience and in a cascade presentation can cause confusion and embarrassment for the presenter.
  • Don‘t mix up different styles through your presentation. Use only one style that is simple and beautiful.
  • Don‘t create your presentation based on what you want to say or what you think your audience wants to know.
  • The best presentations are created based on having asked the audience what they want to know about before you create your presentation. It‘s a little bit of extra work, but the benefits are rich.
  • Don‘t create long bullets with several sentences; try to keep your bullets to single sentences, two maximum.
  • Don‘t use more than six bullets per slide. if you have headers and footers on corporate template, this should be lessened.
  • Don‘t put word for word exactly what you are going to say on your slide. This makes people switch off and they may stop to see either the slide or hear what you are saying. This is a common mistake that people make when presenting and is especially true in cascade presentations where the presenter doesn't know the content and doesn't have good speaker notes to refer to.
  • Don‘t put in meaningless slides, e.g. additional slide titles, agendas on a short presentations, etc.
  • Don‘t make your presentation assume it will never change if you need to present it again. After every presentation you do, think about the questions that came up. With every presentation your delivery should become much slicker and easier if you adopt this process.
  • Don‘t use acronyms or technical terms unless you previously prefaced them.
  • Don‘t try to cram too much information into one slide.
